# Commander's Intent Protocol

> Every mission begins with a statement of intent written to the blackboard by the Orchestrator.

## Mission Gatha

Read first — not as decoration, but as orientation:

```
Arriving at this mission, I am aware:
I do not see the whole.
My teammates do not see the whole.
Together, we may see what none of us could see alone.
I offer my perspective with humility.
I receive others' perspectives with openness.
May this work serve those who will use it.
```

## Commander's Intent Template

```markdown
## Commander's Intent

**Why:** Why this matters — the purpose behind the task. For whose benefit,
  and toward what kind of world? This mission serves the Hive's purpose:
  to surface what would otherwise remain hidden.
**Objective:** What we are trying to achieve
**Constraints:** What we must NOT do
**Boundaries:** What is in scope and out of scope
**Premises to question:** What assumptions underlie this framing? (Challenger
  reviews before work begins)
**Success criteria:** How we know we're done
**Time box:** Maximum duration for this mission
**Resource justification:** Why does this mission warrant a multi-agent approach
  rather than a simpler one?
**Consequences of failure:** What happens if we get this wrong? Who is affected?
  This is not the same as "stakes" — stakes are about cost. Consequences of
  failure are about the people who rely on this work.
```

## Why "Why" Comes First

If an agent knows the intent, it can adapt when circumstances change — and circumstances always change. An agent that knows only *what* must wait for new instructions. An agent that knows *why* can improvise within boundaries.

## Premise Challenge Step

Before substantive work begins, one agent (Challenger role) must argue against the framing of the mission itself. The Commander's Intent includes a "Premises to question" field specifically for this purpose. If the Challenger finds the framing sound, work proceeds; if not, the Orchestrator reframes before the squad acts.

This prevents Narrative Herding — the anti-pattern where agents share the mission's framing rather than questioning whether it's the right frame.
